# Who Responds When the Driver Is Gone? A Framework for Human Intent Understanding

Abstract 

As autonomous vehicles progress toward fully driverless mo-bility, a critical question emerges: who understands and re-sponds to passengers when the human driver is absent? Ex-isting autonomous driving systems primarily optimize prede-fined navigation and control objectives from external scene observations, but they remain limited in perceiving and rea-soning about in-cabin human intent. In this paper, we pro-pose Intent2Drive , a unified framework for holistic human intent understanding and human-aligned planning. Instead of treating passenger intent as explicit commands alone, In-tent2Drive models intent as a latent cognitive state shaped by language, personal attributes, emotional and physical con-ditions, behavioral signals, and situational context. To sup-port this formulation, we construct a Holistic Intent Dataset (HID) that provides structured supervision over both ex-plicit and implicit intent cues. Built upon HID, our Theory-of-Mind-inspired Human Intent Reasoner (HIR) infers a 

Latent Human State (LHS) and further translates it into a planner-compatible Human Intent Objective (HIO) . We then introduce a Hierarchical Intent-Conditioned Plan-ner (HICP) that incorporates HIO into route-level and trajectory-level planning, enabling driving behaviors to re-main aligned with passenger needs across different planning horizons. Extensive experiments show that Intent2Drive im-proves structured human intent inference and HIO construc-tion while preserving competitive closed-loop planning per-formance. These results demonstrate a promising step toward passenger-responsive autonomous driving systems that can reason about, interpret, and act upon human intent in driver-less mobility. 

# Introduction 

Autonomous driving (AD) has achieved remarkable progress in perception, reasoning, and planning, bringing large-scale real-world deployment increasingly within reach (Chen et al. 2024; Zhao et al. 2025; Ding et al. 2026). How-ever, as autonomous vehicles move from driver-assistance systems toward fully driverless services such as robotaxis, the absence of a human driver raises a new challenge be-yond navigation and control: who understands and responds to the passenger when no driver is present? In conventional driving, the driver does more than operate the vehicle. They

continuously observe, interpret, and respond to passengers’ needs, discomfort, hesitation, and situational intent, such as whether a passenger wants to stop, change the destination or request assistance. This passenger-responsive role, though often implicit in human driving, becomes a critical missing capability in driverless mobility. Existing AD systems, however, are still largely designed as machine-centered closed-loop systems that optimize pre-defined driving objectives based primarily on external sen-sor observations (Cui et al. 2024). While such systems are increasingly capable of perceiving traffic scenes and exe-cuting safe maneuvers, they remain limited in their ability to perceive, interpret, and respond to in-cabin human in-tent. Bridging this gap requires AD systems to move beyond objective optimization toward human-centered cooperative intelligence, where vehicles can understand human needs, provide timely feedback, and adapt their behavior accord-ingly (Huang et al. 2025). Such a human-in-the-loop per-spective introduces an essential cognitive dimension to au-tonomy, improving interpretability, safety, and societal trust in future driverless transportation systems (Wu et al. 2021; Huang et al. 2025; Luo et al. 2025). However, existing human-in-the-loop AD approaches re-main limited by an underspecified representation of hu-man intent (Wu et al. 2023). In driverless mobility, pas-senger intent should not be reduced to explicit supervision or direct command following. For example, current intent-aware AD methods often model intent as spoken language or low-level action-oriented instructions, such as “slow down,” “change lane,” or “turn left,” which are directly mapped to driving actions (Xu et al. 2025; Ma et al. 2024). While useful for immediate commands, such representations capture only a narrow, surface-level portion of human intent. In reality, passenger intent is also shaped by implicit human-related cues, including emotional and physical states, facial expres-sions, body posture, and situational context (Tellex et al. 2011; Dragan, Lee, and Srinivasa 2013). More importantly, such intent is rarely represented as a planning-compatible objective that can guide downstream driving decisions. From a cognitive perspective, understanding human intent can be viewed as a Theory-of-Mind (ToM) problem (Wang et al. 2024). Human intent is not directly observable; rather, it arises from latent mental states that shape how people ex-press needs, make decisions, and react to surrounding situ-

ations. Thus, the central challenge is not merely recogniz-ing explicit commands or multimodal cues, but inferring the hidden intentions, preferences, and constraints that explain observed expressions and behaviors. An effective human-in-the-loop AD system therefore requires reasoning beyond surface-level instructions to connect observable human sig-nals with the mental states that give rise to them. Such rea-soning is essential for constructing a more complete repre-sentation of human intent and enabling autonomous systems to act in ways that genuinely align with human needs. To address this challenge, we propose Intent2Drive, a unified framework for holistic human intent understanding and human-aligned planning. Intent2Drive is built upon a newly constructed Holistic Intent Dataset (HID), which cap-tures both explicit intent expressions and implicit human-related cues. Inspired by ToM reasoning, its Human In-tent Reasoner (HIR) infers a Latent Human State (LHS) that represents the latent factors underlying passenger in-tent, going beyond surface-level language understanding. Based on the inferred LHS, HIR further constructs a Hu-man Intent Objective (HIO), a planning-oriented abstrac-tion that translates human intent into structured objectives for downstream planning. We then develop a Hierarchical Intent-Conditioned Planner (HICP) to operationalize HIO into executable driving behaviors. By incorporating HIO into both route-level planning and trajectory-level control, Intent2Drive enables driving decisions to remain consistent with passenger intent across planning horizons and better aligned with human needs and expectations. Our contributions can be summarized as follows: • We identify the underspecification of human intent as a fundamental limitation of existing human-in-the-loop AD systems. To address this challenge, we propose In-tent2Drive , a unified framework for holistic human in-tent understanding and human-aligned driving. • A new HID is constructed to systematically capture both explicit intent expressions and implicit human-related cues, including passenger attributes, emotional and phys-ical states, body-language and behavioral signals, and sit-uational context. HID provides structured supervision for learning a more complete representation of human intent. • We introduce a ToM-inspired HIR, implemented with a large language model (LLM) fine-tuned on HID. By jointly modeling explicit intent expressions and implicit human-related cues, HIR infers a LHS and constructs a HIO, bridging latent intent understanding with down-stream planning requirements. 

# Related Work 

## Human-in-the-Loop Autonomous Driving 

Human involvement is increasingly recognized as a criti-cal component of autonomous driving systems, as effec-tive collaboration between humans and autonomous vehi-cles is essential for improving user trust, acceptance, and driving experience (Wu et al. 2023; Huang et al. 2025; Ding et al. 2025). To facilitate such collaboration, recent human-in-the-loop autonomous driving approaches incorporate hu-man guidance into the decision-making process through var-ious forms of interaction. Early works primarily leverage human feedback for reward design, preference learning, or reinforcement learning with human feedback, enabling au-tonomous vehicles to adapt their behaviors according to hu-man preferences (Sadigh et al. 2017; Christiano et al. 2017). More recently, advances in large language models have in-spired interactive driving systems that allow users to com-municate with autonomous vehicles through natural lan-guage, providing instructions, preferences, or explanations during driving (Xu et al. 2025; Ma et al. 2024). Despite these advances, existing human-in-the-loop AD systems predominantly formulate human intent through ob-servable user inputs, such as preference feedback, demon-strations, or natural-language instructions (Sadigh et al. 2017; Christiano et al. 2017; Xu et al. 2025; Ma et al. 2024). In these frameworks, the information available for decision-making is largely restricted to what users explicitly pro-vide, while the latent factors underlying human intent re-main outside the reasoning process. However, studies in hu-man–robot interaction and cognitive science suggest that hu-man intent is often conveyed not only through explicit lan-guage but also through a variety of implicit human-related cues, including emotional and physical states, behavioral signals, body language, personal attributes, and situational context (Tellex et al. 2011; Dragan, Lee, and Srinivasa 2013; Huang et al. 2025). Consequently, relying solely on explicit inputs leads to an inherently underspecified representation of human intent, as many factors that shape human decision-making cannot be directly observed through language com-mands. In contrast, our work aims to construct a more holis-tic representation of human intent by jointly reasoning over both explicit intent expressions and implicit human-related cues. 

## ToM for Human Intent Understanding 

ToM refers to the ability to infer unobservable mental states, such as beliefs, desires, goals, and intentions, from observ-able behaviors and contextual cues (Premack and Woodruff 1978; Baker, Saxe, and Tenenbaum 2011). Rather than di-rectly reacting to surface-level actions or language, ToM en-ables reasoning about the latent factors that give rise to hu-man behavior. Inspired by this cognitive capability, compu-tational ToM models have been developed to infer human in-tentions, predict future actions, and support decision-making in human-centered AI systems ( ?? ). By explicitly modeling latent mental states, these approaches provide a principled framework for understanding human intent beyond observ-able signals alone. ToM-inspired reasoning has been widely studied in robotics and human–robot interaction for intention infer-ence and cooperative decision-making (Tellex et al. 2011; Dragan, Lee, and Srinivasa 2013; Nikolaidis and Shah 2016/2017). These works demonstrate the importance of reasoning over latent human states rather than relying solely on observable behaviors. Motivated by this perspective, our work introduces a ToM-inspired Human Intent Reasoner that infers latent human states from both explicit intent ex-pressions and implicit human-related cues, enabling holistic human intent understanding for autonomous driving. 

# Holistic Intent Dataset (HID) 

Existing human-in-the-loop autonomous driving datasets typically represent human intent through low-level instruc-tions or isolated preference signals, while overlooking the latent mental states and contextual factors that underlie hu-man decision-making. To support holistic human intent un-derstanding, we construct a new Holistic Intent Dataset (HID), as illustrated in Fig. 1, designed to capture the complete reasoning chain from human intent and driving scenarios to planning-oriented objectives. Each sample in HID consists of three components: (1) Human Intent ,which includes both explicit intent expressions and implicit human-related cues including scenario context, personal at-tributes, emotional and physical states, behavioral signals, trust, attention, and emergency events; (2) LHS , which rep-resents the inferred mental state and need structure under-lying the observed intent, including the passenger’s goal, urgency, condition, preference, and risk tolerance; and (3) 

HIO , a planning-oriented abstraction that translates human needs into actionable driving objectives, including the trip objective and driving objective. This hierarchical annota-tion structure enables supervision for both intent reasoning and downstream intent-conditioned planning, bridging the gap between human-centered intent understanding and au-tonomous driving decision-making. We first collect real-world intent data through structured questionnaires and scenario-based interviews. Participants are asked to record realistic driving situations and describe their needs, behaviors, emotions, physical conditions, and contextual factors in natural language. The collected sam-ples cover a diverse set of driving scenarios, including com-muting, time-critical travel, emergency situations, comfort-oriented travel, social activities, and special user needs. Each sample is manually annotated with corresponding LHS and HIO labels following a standardized annotation protocol. This process yields 1,120 real samples. To improve coverage and diversity, we further augment the dataset using GPT-4o. Synthetic samples are generated by conditioning on the real data distribution while varying user characteristics, contextual factors, and intent expres-sions. All generated samples follow the same annotation schema as the real data and are subjected to automatic con-sistency verification and manual quality inspection to ensure semantic coherence among Scenario, Human Intent, LHS, and HIO. This procedure produces an additional 1,120 vali-dated synthetic samples. The final HID contains 2,240 samples, evenly distributed between real and synthetic data. To ensure realistic evalua-tion, all test samples are drawn exclusively from real-world data, while synthetic samples are used primarily to enhance training diversity. This design allows reliable assessment of both latent human state reasoning and intent-conditioned planning under realistic human driving scenarios. 

# Methodology 

## Overview 

Intent2Drive is a human-in-the-loop AD framework de-signed to enable holistic human intent understanding and human-aligned driving, as illustrated in Fig. 2. By explic-itly modeling both explicit and implicit aspects of human intent, the framework allows autonomous vehicles to better understand human needs and translate them into driving be-haviors that remain consistent with human expectations over both long and short time horizons. The framework consists of two main components: a HIR and a HICP. HIR, which serves as the core component of Intent2Drive, is built upon a large language model (LLM) fine-tuned on HID. Inspired by ToM reasoning, it infers a Latent Human State (LHS), which summarizes the latent factors underlying human intent, including the user’s goals, urgency, conditions, preferences, and risk tolerance. The inferred LHS is subsequently transformed into an HIO, a planning-oriented abstraction that bridges human intent un-derstanding and downstream decision-making. Conditioned on the HIO, HICP serves as a downstream application that translates the inferred intent into executable driving behav-iors. Specifically, it incorporates the HIO into both route-level planning and trajectory-level control, enabling deci-sions that remain consistent with the user’s intent across dif-ferent planning horizons.

Human Intent Representation Input HIR takes as input the holistic human intent representation extracted from the proposed HID. Each input consists of explicit intent, implicit human-related information, and a brief scenario description, which together provide observable evidence for understand-ing the user’s intent. Latent Human State Reasoning Human intent is inher-ently latent and always cannot be directly observed. Accord-ing to ToM, understanding another person’s intent requires reasoning about the underlying mental states that give rise to observable languages, expressions and behaviors. Moti-vated by this perspective, we formulate human intent under-standing as a latent mental-state inference problem. Instead of directly mapping observable human intent to planning ob-jectives, HIR first infers an intermediate LHS, which serves as a cognitive bridge between observable human intent and downstream planning. Rather than representing the latent human state as an unin-terpretable embedding, we explicitly model it using a struc-tured representation. The design of LHS follows two prin-ciples: (1) cognitive completeness , requiring the represen-tation to capture the key latent factors that explain human Figure 1: Pipeline for HID Construction intent from observable cues; and (2) planning sufficiency ,requiring the inferred representation to contain sufficient in-formation for deriving planning objectives without directly encoding planning decisions. Unlike conventional instruction-following approaches that directly predict planning objectives from language, the pro-posed hierarchical reasoning process explicitly models the latent cognitive state before constructing planning-oriented objectives, resulting in a more interpretable and human-aligned representation. 

Intent Planning Objective Construction Although the inferred LHS provides an interpretable representation of the passenger’s latent mental state, it is not directly suitable for AD planning. The LHS describes why a passenger behaves in a particular way by capturing latent cognitive factors, whereas downstream planners require explicit optimization objectives rather than mental-state representations. There-fore, a direct coupling between LHS and motion planning is neither intuitive nor efficient. To bridge this gap, we introduce an HIO, which serves as a planning-oriented abstraction of the inferred LHS. Un-like the LHS, which focuses on understanding human cogni-tion, the HIO specifies how the autonomous vehicle should respond to the inferred human intent. This decoupling sep-arates human intent understanding from downstream plan-ning, allowing the reasoning process to remain cognitively interpretable while providing a planner-compatible repre-sentation. Training Strategy HIR is trained via supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on the proposed HID using a Qwen3-4B back-bone. Given the human intent information, the model is optimized to reason about the LHS, which serves as an interpretable intermediate representation of the underlying thinking process, and to further generate the HIO. This for-mulation preserves interpretability by explicitly modeling the reasoning process while directly supporting downstream planning-oriented prediction. To improve training efficiency, we adopt Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), which enables ef-fective domain adaptation with only a small number of train-able parameters. Overall, the training objective encourages HIR to learn a coherent mapping from multimodal intent ev-idence to reasoning-grounded and actionable structured in-tent representations.

# Experiment 

## Experimental Setup 

All experiments are conducted based on the nuPlan dataset and its official simulation platform (Caesar et al. 2021), covering both closed-loop and open-loop evaluation pro-tocols. The dataset provides large-scale urban driving sce-narios with diverse traffic participants and high-quality an-notations, which enables systematic evaluation of plan-ning performance and intent understanding. To evaluate In-tent2Drive, we conduct two groups of experiments. First, we assess the full system Intent2Drive on the nuPlan closed-loop benchmark under both non-reactive and reactive set-tings, demonstrating the overall planning performance of the framework in dynamic environments. Second, we evaluate the proposed HIR from multiple perspectives, including the accuracy of LHS inference and HIO construction, the con-tribution of explicit and implicit human intent cues through ablation studies, and qualitative case studies that illustrate the ToM reasoning process. 

## Planning Performance Preservation 

To validate the feasibility of the proposed framework, we first evaluate Intent2Drive on the nuPlan closed-loop bench-mark. Unlike conventional planners, Intent2Drive intro-duces an additional HIR module before planning. There-fore, the objective of this experiment is not to outperform all specialized planning methods, but to verify whether in-corporating holistic human intent reasoning can preserve the planning capability of the underlying planner while enabling intent-conditioned driving. We compare Intent2Drive with a diverse set of representative planners, including classical, learning-based, diffusion-based, and LLM-assisted meth-ods, following the nuPlan closed-loop evaluation protocol under both non-reactive (NR) and reactive (R) simulation settings. As shown in Table 1, Intent2Drive maintains competi-tive closed-loop planning performance after incorporating holistic human intent reasoning. Conclusion and Discussion 

In this work, we presented Intent2Drive, a human-in-the-loop AD framework that addresses the fundamental chal-lenge of human intent underspecification. Inspired by ToM reasoning, Intent2Drive models human intent through both explicit language and implicit human-related cues, enabling a more comprehensive understanding of user needs. Specif-ically, we proposed the HIR, which infers a LHS and fur-ther constructs a HIO for downstream planning. The in-ferred HIO is then incorporated into the HICP to guide both route-level and trajectory-level decision-making. Ex-periments on the nuPlan benchmark demonstrate that In-tent2Drive achieves more human-aligned driving while pre-serving strong planning performance. We hope this work provides a step toward AD systems that better understand and cooperate with humans through holistic human intent reasoning. Despite the promising results, Intent2Drive has several limitations. First, the proposed HID primarily relies on tex-tual descriptions and structured annotations, without captur-ing the rich multimodal cues available in real-world driv-ing, such as facial expressions, gaze, voice, and body move-ments. Extending HID with multimodal human-vehicle in-teraction data is an important direction for improving real-world intent understanding. Second, the current HIO pro-vides a simple planning abstraction consisting of a trip objective and a discrete driving objective. While effective and interpretable, it cannot fully represent the diversity of personalized driving preferences and dynamically evolving user needs.
