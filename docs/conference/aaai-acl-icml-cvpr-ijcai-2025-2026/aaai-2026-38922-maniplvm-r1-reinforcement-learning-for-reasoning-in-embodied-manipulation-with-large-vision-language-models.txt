Abstract 

Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have recently ad-vanced robotic manipulation by leveraging vision for scene perception and language for instruction following. However, existing methods rely heavily on costly human-annotated training datasets, which limits their generalization and causes them to struggle in out-of-domain (OOD) scenarios, re-ducing real-world adaptability. To address these challenges, we propose ManipLVM-R1, a novel reinforcement learning framework that replaces traditional supervision with Rein-forcement Learning using Verifiable Rewards (RLVR). By directly optimizing for task-aligned outcomes, our method enhances generalization and physical reasoning while re-moving the dependence on costly annotations. Specifically, we design two rule-based reward functions targeting key robotic manipulation subtasks: an Affordance Perception Re-ward to enhance localization of interaction regions, and a Trajectory Match Reward to ensure the physical plausibil-ity of action paths. These rewards provide immediate feed-back and impose spatial-logical constraints, encouraging the model to go beyond shallow pattern matching and instead learn deeper, more systematic reasoning about physical in-teractions. Experimental results show that ManipLVM-R1 achieves substantial performance gains across multiple ma-nipulation tasks, using only 50% of the training data while achieving strong generalization to OOD scenarios. We fur-ther analyze the benefits of our reward design and its impact on task success and efficiency.

## Related Work 

RLVR for Large Vision Language Models 

Large Vision-Language Models (LVMs) have demonstrated impressive reasoning capabilities across a variety of visual tasks (Li et al. 2024a; Wang et al. 2024a; Bai et al. 2023; Wang et al. 2024b). Their ability to align visual percep-tion with language understanding has enabled strong perfor-mance in domains such as question answering, captioning, and instruction following. To further enhance these capabilities, recent research has explored Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Re-wards (RLVR). Vision-R1 (Huang et al. 2025a) introduces the PTST strategy, allowing effective training on cold-start datasets while matching performance with significantly larger models. LMM-R1 (Peng et al. 2025) employs a two-stage RLVR pipeline, leveraging text-only reasoning in the first stage before transferring to vision-based domains, achieving effective modality transfer. VLM-R1 (Shen et al. 2025) adapts the RLVR paradigm to open-vocabulary object detection by grounding language in visual entities through verifiable feedback. These approaches show that RLVR can effectively activate latent reasoning capabilities within pre-trained models, even without extensive human supervision. However, none of these directly address the domain of robotic manipulation, which requires not just perception and reasoning but also physical execution and control. To bridge this gap, we propose ManipLVM-R1, an RLVR-based framework tailored to manipulation tasks, integrating both high-level reasoning and low-level action grounding. 

Robot Manipulation 

Traditional robot manipulation methods often relied on low-level state-based reinforcement learning (Geng et al. 2023), which struggled to generalize to complex, high-dimensional visual inputs. The recent integration of LLMs and vision-language models into manipulation pipelines has led to a new wave of vision-based manipulation approaches (Bro-han et al. 2022, 2023a; Wang et al. 2023; Li et al. 2024b; Liu et al. 2024c), enabling more abstract task planning and generalization. Some methods aim to ground language into spatial repre-sentations. VoxPoser (Huang et al. 2023a) synthesizes 3D volumetric value maps from textual instructions to guide object placement. Others focus on fine-tuning VLMs for robotic tasks. RoboFlamingo (Li et al. 2023b) and Mani-pLLM (Li et al. 2024b) supervise models with CoT data to learn visual reasoning for manipulation. RoboMamba (Liu et al. 2024c) and OpenVLA (Kim et al. 2024) further extend this by curating high-quality datasets for language-guided visual planning. Embodied Reasoner (Zhang et al. 2025), Cosmos-Reason1 (Azzolini et al. 2025), and RoboBrain (Ji et al. 2025c) push the boundary by improving interpretability and 18559 reasoning alignment through supervised CoT fine-tuning. Despite these advances, most approaches still depend heavily on large-scale annotations and handcrafted supervi-sion strategies. In contrast, our ManipLVM-R1 framework eliminates the need for extensive CoT labels, instead learn-ing from structured, verifiable rewards. This enables robust reasoning and generalization under low supervision condi-tions, setting the stage for more scalable and autonomous robot learning. 

## Method 

In this section, we first present the preliminaries of RLVR. Subsequently, we introduce the training framework for ManipLVM-R1 and elucidate the rationale behind its design. A visual overview of our architecture is shown in Figure 2. 

Preliminary of Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards 

Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) (Guo et al. 2025; Team et al. 2025b) is a paradigm for training language or vision-language models on tasks where correctness can be automatically verified—such as in mathematics, programming, and increasingly, embodied AI. Unlike reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) (Liu et al. 2024a; Zang et al. 2025), RLVR removes the need for subjective preference labels by relying on task-specific verification functions to determine reward signals. Formally, given an instruction q, a policy model πθ gen-erates a candidate response o, which is then evaluated by a verifiable reward function R(q, o ). The model is trained to maximize the following RLVR objective:

ManipLVM-R1 

While RLVR has shown promise in domains like mathemat-ics and programming, its application to robotic manipula-tion remains limited. This is primarily due to two challenges: (1) sparse and delayed reward signals in manipulation tasks, which hinder effective learning and optimization, and (2) the lack of spatial-logical constraints in reward design, causing models to rely on superficial pattern matching rather than learning grounded physical reasoning. To address these is-sues, we propose ManipLVM-R1, an RLVR-based frame-work that incorporates structured reward design and stable policy optimization. ManipLVM-R1 decomposes the overall manipulation task into two distinct subtasks: affordance per-ception and trajectory prediction. It trains separate models for each subtask, enabling more focused learning and better generalization in physically grounded environments.

Given an image and an instruction, the policy model generates multiple responses, which are optimized using structured rewards: the Affordance Perception Reward for affordance detection, and the Trajectory Match Reward based on multiple distance metrics, path similarity, and endpoint accuracy. To encourage the model to generate physically plausible and goal-aligned trajectories, we design the path similarity reward Rpath . The motivation behind this design is that many supervised methods, such as behavioral cloning (Nazeri and Bohlouli 2021), focus primarily on endpoint accuracy while overlooking the quality and feasibility of the trajectory it-self. In robotic manipulation tasks, the shape, continuity, and alignment of the trajectory with respect to the ground truth are critical for ensuring robust and safe control strategies. To capture these aspects, we incorporate multiple geometric similarity metrics that evaluate how well the predicted tra-jectory matches the ground truth from different perspectives. 

Rpath is a weighted aggregation of rewards derived from three distinct metrics: Discrete Fr´ echet Distance (DFD), Hausdorff Distance (HD), and Root Mean Square Error (RMSE). The reward for each metric ( RDFD , R HD , R RMSE )is obtained by normalizing the respective distance value to a score between 0 and 1. RDFD quantifies overall shape sim-ilarity and temporal alignment between trajectories; RHD 

captures the maximum pointwise deviation; RRMSE mea-sures the average pointwise discrepancy. The final Rpath is as: 

Rpath = RDFD + RHD + RRMSE . (4) The endpoint distance reward Rend measures the proxim-ity between the final predicted point ˆpN and the ground-truth endpoint pM using Euclidean distance: Rend =exp( −k|| ˆpN − p∗

M || 2). The overall trajectory match reward is defined as the sum of the three components: 

Rtrajectory = Rformat + Rpath + Rend . (5) 

Policy Update Inspired by Group Relative Policy Opti-mization (GRPO) (Guo et al. 2025), we select multiple re-sponses from the current policy as candidate actions. De-pending on the task type, either Rspatial or Rtrajectory assigns a scalar reward ri to each response, resulting in a reward set 

{r1, r 2, . . . , r G}. To assess the quality of each response rel-ative to others, we normalize the rewards by computing the mean and standard deviation: 

Ai = ri − mean( {r1, . . . , r G})std( {r1, . . . , r G}) , (6) where Ai denotes the advantage of the i-th response. These advantages are then used to update the policy, increasing the likelihood of high-quality responses while suppress-ing lower-quality ones. To ensure stability during train-ing, the update is constrained by minimizing the KL diver-gence between the updated policy and a reference model: 

KL [ πθ (o|q) ∥ πref (o|q)] .

Dataset Setting 

In-Domain Dataset All baseline and proposed models are trained on the ShareRobot (Ji et al. 2025c) dataset, a large-scale resource curated from Open X-Embodiment (O’Neill et al. 2024). It is tailored for fine-grained robotic manipu-lation tasks including affordance perception and trajectory generation. The dataset includes over 1 million planning QA pairs, 6.5k images with dense affordance annotations, and 6.8k trajectory-labeled frames spanning 102 real-world scenes and 12 robotic embodiments. Rich multimodal su-pervision provides an ideal testbed for learning spatial and temporal reasoning. Importantly, all SFT baselines use the full training set, while ManipLVM-R1 trains on only 50% of the data, demonstrating strong sample efficiency. 

Out-of-Domain Dataset To evaluate the robustness and generalization of our model, we conduct extensive out-of-domain (OOD) testing. For affordance perception, we select four semantic categories ( grasp , cut , pound , scoop ) from the UMD Part Affordance dataset (Nguyen et al. 2017). This dataset provides RGB-D images and 3D object point clouds covering 105 common tools across household and industrial contexts. We randomly sample 1200 test images from the four categories with equal distribution, ensuring diverse cov-erage in geometry and affordance semantics. For trajectory prediction, we use a subset of 500 exam-ples from the validation split of VAIT (Niu et al. 2024), de-rived from the Open X-Embodiment corpus. VAIT consists of image-trajectory pairs capturing a broad distribution of robot behaviors and environments. However, as it is auto-matically mined, it contains noisy or misaligned trajectory labels. To address this, we visualize all selected examples and apply manual verification and correction of annotations where necessary, ensuring that the OOD evaluation remains clean and reliable. This careful curation enables precise as-sessment of model performance under distribution shift and perceptual uncertainty. 

Metric Setting 

Affordance Perception: We report task success rate as the primary evaluation metric, where a prediction is consid-ered successful if the Intersection over Union (IoU) with the ground truth exceeds 50%. This threshold-based crite-rion reflects whether the model can accurately localize the functional region relevant to the task. In addition, we include mean IoU as an auxiliary metric to capture the average spa-tial alignment quality, which provides finer-grained insight into partial matches and near misses. 

Trajectory Prediction: Success is defined as the final pre-dicted waypoint falling within a 20-pixel Euclidean dis-tance of the ground truth endpoint, reflecting whether the action can reach the intended goal. To capture global tra-jectory similarity and path consistency, we also report three alignment metrics—Discrete Fr´ echet Distance (DFD), Hausdorff Distance (HD), and Root Mean Square Error (RMSE)—following (Chao et al. 2021; Wang et al. 2024a; Ji et al. 2025c). DFD measures overall shape resemblance and temporal coherence, HD identifies the worst-case devia-tion between two trajectories, and RMSE quantifies average pointwise error. Quantitative Analysis 

We evaluate the performance of ManipLVM-R1-3B on both affordance perception and trajectory prediction tasks under in-domain and OOD settings. In the in-domain experiment, our model achieves an IoU of 31.0 on the affordance task, substantially outperform-ing all baselines, including the much larger RoboBrain-7B (11.79), despite using only 50% of the training data. This un-derscores the effectiveness of our structured reward design in generating spatially grounded and semantically meaning-ful predictions under limited supervision, validating our mo-tivation to align learning signals directly with task utility. For trajectory prediction, ManipLVM-R1 also demon-strates strong performance, with an average endpoint error of 110.87 which comparable to fully supervised, larger mod-els indicating that our reward formulation captures core re-quirements for physical feasibility and goal-directed plausi-bility, as intended. In the OOD experiment, ManipLVM-R1 continues to ex-hibit robust generalization. It achieves the highest Grasp-IoU (34.65) and competitive results across other UMD sub-tasks. More notably, it obtains the lowest average trajectory error (131.99) on the VAIT benchmark, outperforming all open-source and fine-tuned models, including Qwen2.5-VL-32B-Instruct and RoboBrain-7B. These results collectively demonstrate ManipLVM-R1’s ability to generalize under distribution shifts while consis-tently maintaining high task accuracy across both affordance perception and trajectory prediction domains.

## Conclusion 

We present ManipLVM-R1, a reinforcement learning frame-work that replaces costly human supervision with verifi-able, rule-based rewards for robotic manipulation. By in-troducing task-aligned reward functions for affordance per-ception and trajectory prediction, our method enhances sys-tematic reasoning and generalization, particularly in out-of-domain settings. Unlike prior annotation-heavy approaches, ManipLVM-R1 uses immediate structured feedback to guide learning, enabling shorter inference paths, higher decision efficiency, and improved robustness under limited supervi-sion. Experiments show that ManipLVM-R1 reduces super-vision costs while maintaining strong performance with only 50% of the training data, illustrating a scalable direction for efficient robot learning.
